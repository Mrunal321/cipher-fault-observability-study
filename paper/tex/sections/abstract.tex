Gate-level fault studies often inject a bit flip at each gate output and
measure how often the fault reaches a primary output. This comparison can
be misleading when the synthesis flows use gates of different sizes. In
simple terms, we ask whether LUT, AIG, and MIG netlists really change the
measured fault behavior, or whether the change mostly comes from counting
different kinds of gate sites. Using 15 cipher primitives from PRESENT,
GIFT, PRINCE, SIMON, Keccak-$\chi$, and Speck/ARX, we compare
truth-table LUT/AOIG, 2-input AIG, and MIG representations under the same
gate-output \bitflip{} model. The LUT/AOIG-to-MIG comparison suggests a
benefit: MIG reduces average per-vector detection probability (\AvgDP{})
by 13.0\% on average. Under a decomposed-AIG baseline at comparable
small-gate granularity, however, MIG \emph{increases} \AvgDP{} by
21.4\%, with no circuit showing a reduction. As a granularity control,
we restrict faults to output-driving gates. Target-layer \AvgDP{} is
$1.000$ across all three representations, showing that the all-site
difference comes from interior gate sites rather than the output-driving
layer. One finding remains stable across representation: ARX primitives
exhibit about $1.9\times$ broader output corruption per detected fault
than substitution-based primitives. We give reporting guidelines for
synthesis-flow fault metrics and release the netlists, campaign scripts,
generated tables, and scripts that check the quoted summary numbers.
